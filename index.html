GHOSTRADAR: A Framework for Angle-Domain Sensing Manipulation in Automotive FMCW Radars

Submitted to IEEE S&P 2026 Anonymized

Abstract

Frequency modulated continuous wave (FMCW) radars play a vital role for modern advanced driver assistance systems (ADAS) because of their robust sensing capabilities. However, they remain vulnerable to signal spoofing attacks. These attacks can cause vehicles to detect nonexistent objects, potentially triggering improper braking or evasive maneuvers. Although prior research has demonstrated radar spoofing attacks, existing approaches primarily focus on generating fake objects at a fixed distance directly in front of a vehicle, lacking angle manipulation. Therefore, the attack scenario is limited to frontal, fixed-range deception and cannot place targets at arbitrary azimuths.

In this work, we unleash the full potential of radar spoofing attacks by introducing GHOSTRADAR, a framework that crafts spoofing signals for a victim automotive radar to create controllable static fake objects at arbitrary angles and distances. We develop a ray-tracing-based mmWave radar simulator that accurately models environmental multipath reflections and use it to rigorously evaluate GHOSTRADAR. Large-scale simulations show that GHOSTRADAR achieves an average distance error of 0.45 meters and an average angular error of 2.67 degrees. Then, we run end-to-end attack evaluation in CARLA-based driving scenarios. The results show that 6 attack modes at 106 locations on three maps cause abnormal driving decisions in over 80% of trials. Finally, we build an attack prototype with commodity mmWave radars that validates GHOSTRADAR under real-world conditions. These results reveal precise, angle-aware spoofing capabilities and expose critical vulnerabilities in automotive radar systems.

Introduction

Figure 1: Overview of the GHOSTRADAR attack scenario.

Modern autonomous driving systems rely heavily on automotive FMCW radars due to their robustness under adverse weather and illumination conditions. However, their physical-layer design makes them vulnerable to spoofing, where an attacker injects carefully crafted signals to create false objects in the radar’s range–angle domain. Existing spoofing techniques are limited to fixed-distance, frontal deception and lack precise angle manipulation, restricting their impact on realistic driving scenarios.

GHOSTRADAR overcomes these limitations by enabling controllable generation of static fake objects at arbitrary angles and distances. As shown in Figure 1, an attacker positioned roadside can fabricate angle-consistent false obstacles that mislead the perception system and induce abnormal motion planning outcomes. This capability is validated through ray-tracing simulation, closed-loop CARLA evaluation, and real-world radar hardware.

Threat Model

Figure 2: Attack goal.

We consider an external wireless adversary equipped with a directional mmWave transmitter that can infer the victim FMCW radar’s configuration, synchronize spoofing chirps with microsecond accuracy, and beamform coherent signals to create static fake objects at attacker-chosen ranges and angles that remain fixed in the world frame as the ego vehicle moves. By strategically placing these virtual obstacles in the radar range–angle map (e.g., blocking adjacent lanes, steering the vehicle toward specific regions, or obstructing turning paths at intersections), the attacker can manipulate high-level driving decisions without compromising any in-vehicle software or other sensors.

System Design

Figure 3: System design of GHOSTRADAR.

GHOSTRADAR is an end-to-end framework that takes attacker-specified fake object locations in the world frame, computes their range, angle, and Doppler signatures relative to the moving ego vehicle, and converts them into per-chirp spoofing signals aligned with the victim FMCW radar’s configuration. A ray-tracing mmWave radar simulator generates realistic multi-path reflections and FMCW ADC samples, which are combined with spoofed echoes and injected into a CARLA-based autonomous driving stack to systematically evaluate angle-aware spoofing across diverse maps, attack locations, and driving tasks.

Evaluation Setup

We evaluate GHOSTRADAR in three setups: an open-loop radar simulation with CARLA replays to verify that spoofed signals create fake objects at the intended ranges and angles, a closed-loop CARLA-based autonomous driving stack to study how angle-aware spoofing affects perception, planning, and control, and a real-world deployment with commodity automotive radars and a synchronized spoofing transmitter.

In open loop, we measure range and azimuth errors and the detection rate of spoofed peaks; in closed loop, we compare baseline and attacked trajectories and quantify task failures and abnormal behaviors; in real-world tests, we check whether the spoofed target consistently appears at the intended range–angle bin and remains clearly distinguishable from clutter.

Experimental Results

Open-Loop Results

Figure 4: Open-loop ray-tracing based radar simulation.

Figure 5: Open-loop CARLA-based evaluation.

In the open-loop setting, GHOSTRADAR is evaluated independently of any driving policy to verify that spoofed signals create physically plausible and accurately positioned fake objects. As shown in Figures 4 and 5, the ray-tracing radar simulator and CARLA radar pipeline both reconstruct clear static peaks at the commanded ranges and angles, with sub-meter range error and only a few degrees of angular deviation.

These results demonstrate that GHOSTRADAR reliably generates stable, angle-aware fake objects that survive the full sensing pipeline and appear indistinguishable from legitimate radar returns at the perception layer.
